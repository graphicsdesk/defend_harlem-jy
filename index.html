Meet the New York Interfaith Commission, Harlem’s advocate for affordable housing

From organizing public demonstrations to launching the Defend Harlem media platform, the commission has been working to address community concerns in West Harlem for the past 14 years.

By Thomas Stewart

Graphic by Jenny Ye

February 10, 2025

Photo courtesy of Corrie Aune

Thirty years ago, West Harlem tenants lost the Harlem Urban Development Corporation, one of the area’s most prominent advocates for housing equality. In its absence, a new organization of community leaders has stepped up as the community’s “gatekeeper”: the New York Interfaith Commission for Housing Equality.

Over the past 14 years, the organization has cemented itself as a leader in the fight for housing equality. Composed of local politicians, religious leaders, and community members, the commission aims to create affordable housing opportunities for the Harlem community.

The commission’s various political activities range from holding teach-ins to protesting Columbia’s expansion efforts.

Last year, the commission launched Defend Harlem, a new media branch. Since then, Defend Harlem has increased its community presence, hosting several rallies and demonstrations.

[Read More: Defend Harlem holds rally against Columbia’s expansion in Harlem]

Defend Harlem is dedicated to broadcasting the commission’s political goals through storytelling, according to Kai Cogsville, the communications director for the commission. His father, Donald Cogsville, was the former president and founding member of the Harlem Urban Development Corporation before New York Governor George Pataki shut down the organization in 1995.

“Seems like Harlem lost its gatekeeper in terms of community leaders that were really trying to work with different politicians and local leaders to slow down gentrification and preserve the community,” Kai Cogsville said, speaking on the motivations behind the media branch’s creation.

Following the loss of the Harlem Urban Development Corporation, the launch of Defend Harlem is just one of the commission’s ongoing actions in the fight for affordable housing.

Housing affordability in Harlem

The lost “gatekeeper” was Harlem Urban Development Corporation, established in 1970. The organization was responsible for numerous housing and development projects in Harlem, including the Apollo Theater, the 125th Street redesign, and Harlem on the Hudson.

In addition to spearheading projects that generated economic growth for Harlem, it acted as a bridge between elected officials, community leaders, organizations, and businesses.

Given the extensive influence that the HUDC had during its years of operation, the loss of the organization meant the community “didn’t have protection,” according to Donald Cogsville. He said Pataki’s actions as governor negatively impacted the Harlem community.

“He announced his first act of duty would be to close HUDC,” Cogsville said. “They closed it because they said it was too powerful, that’s what he said. And so when he closed it, that meant the elected officials and others did not have the protection of that kind of staff to provide information about what was going on.”

Pataki’s administration cited mismanagement of taxpayer dollars during HUDC’s activity as justification for its shutdown. Though HUDC was never prosecuted for these allegations, the Pataki administration insisted on public exposure of these claims.

In the wake of the HUDC shutdown, the election of former Mayor Michael Bloomberg further complicated affordable housing in Harlem. Following his 2002 election, Bloomberg rezoned 40 percent of New York City, which priced out masses of city residents in Midtown, leading many to buy properties across the city—including in Harlem.

The rezoning in turn pushed out many Harlem residents, and by the 2010s, residents had begun to feel the effects of gentrification, according to Dedrick Blue, Vice Chair for the New York Interfaith Commission.

“For the first time in Harlem, the numbers began to shift so dramatically that Blacks were no longer in the majority of the population in Central Harlem,” Blue said. “We started seeing the decline in church attendance as more and more of our people were forced to move away.”

Though the increase in area median income levels has been responsible for shifts in Harlem demographics, “Race is always involved, no matter how you look at it,” Charles Curtis, president of the commission, said.

The decrease in the Black population in Harlem inspired Curtis to have more conversations about the rapid gentrification in the area. Curtis’ extensive background serving on the board for the Harlem Congregations for Community Improvement—one of the largest affordable housing developers in the country—paired with his work as a senior pastor of the local Mt. Olivet Baptist Church led him to establish what would come to be known as the New York Interfaith Commission in 2011.

The Origins of NYIC

Beginning with just a few founding members including Donald Cogsville, Tariq Shahid, John Scott, and Dedrick Blue, the organization has expanded, seeking to serve, educate, and protect the Harlem community.

Working with local politicians has been a key point of the NYIC mission since its founding. In 2013, NYIC organized a mayoral debate at the Schomburg Center for Research in Black Culture in Harlem to bring the issue of affordable housing to the attention of mayoral candidates. The debate led then-mayoral candidate Bill de Blasio to address the issue during his campaign, ultimately raising awareness that extended beyond the mayoral race.

“He picked up that ball and ran on that,” Blue said. “And then the other groups who had been out there fighting for quite some time on issues of affordability—they began to speak louder and louder until the little streams became a mighty chorus.”

The commission has also focused on holding public demonstrations and drafting proposed legislation. More recently, NYIC has been working with State Sen. Cordell Cleare to develop legislative protections for affordable housing, and NYIC recently supported Sen. Cleare’s proposed bill to stop Columbia’s expansion in West Harlem.

District 9 City Council member Yusef Salaam and District 30 State Sen. Cordell Cleare speak at a protest organized by the NYIC. Photo courtesy of Corrie Aune

Aside from connecting with political leadership, the commission has made an effort to engage with other community organizations and members, primarily through Defend Harlem. Kai Cogsville started the platform with the intent to act as a liaison for the community, distributing information through content and videos. In conjunction with the development of Defend Harlem, the NYIC has expressed a commitment to ongoing dialogue with Columbia.

Contact With Columbia

The NYIC has recently focused much of its energy on Columbia’s expansion into West Harlem, often working hand in hand with students to organize protests.

Defend Harlem and Columbia students rallied against University gentrification in Sept. 2024. Photo courtesy of Corrie Aune

In February 2024, Blue wrote a letter to the University requesting a meeting to address the issue of gentrification and displacement of residents in Upper Manhattan. According to Blue, the University never responded to the initial letter and later said it was never received.

“Columbia continues to prioritize engagement with our local community as we fully realize the full vision of our Manhattanville campus in West Harlem,” a Columbia spokesperson wrote in a statement to Spectator.

The spokesperson wrote that future expansion would be utilized for both academic and community-based programming.

“We welcome any opportunity to engage with local stakeholders and look forward to continuing our dialogue with the New York Interfaith Commission and other groups to hear their ideas for how the University can address the needs and priorities of the local communities,” the spokesperson wrote.

Blue later sent another meeting request, and by November 2024, the University had responded and began dialogue with the NYIC regarding the “encroachment” of the University into the Harlem community, Blue said.

“We actually did have a meeting with them yesterday,” Blue said. “Over the issues of displacement, the encroachment of the University on these historical communities, the issues of affordability in terms of housing, and also issues around who develops all of this. How does all this come to be? How do we protect and preserve what is?”

The One Harlem Coalition, composed of organizations including NYIC, Community Board 9, WE ACT for Environmental Justice, and the Morningside Heights Community Coalition, has gathered to stop the University’s expansion and foster a dialogue about the community’s needs.

According to a University official, Columbia representatives have been meeting regularly with the One Harlem Coalition since fall 2024. The coalition plans to host its next meeting Feb. 19.

NYIC feels hopeful that this dialogue will be constructive for the community, according to Blue.

Community members share their stories at an NYIC community meeting held in April 2024. Photo courtesy of Corrie Aune

Blue said the NYIC is looking forward to continuing its advocacy for housing equality and community building in a cooperative manner in the future.

“We leave a legacy,” Curtis said. “If I can help some young person and get them out of a shelter into a house, if I can educate someone who might not otherwise be educated. … Right now my goal is I’m trying to build the world I want to live in.”
